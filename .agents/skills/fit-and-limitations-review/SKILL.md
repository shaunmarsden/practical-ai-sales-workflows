# Fit and Limitations Review

> Landed here directly rather than clicking through from a guide? This file is the instruction sheet an AI assistant follows, not written for a first read start to finish. [What is a sales AI skill?](../../../guides/what-is-a-sales-ai-skill.md) has the plain-English version.

A business case built on every use case a prospect mentions, rather than the ones that actually fit, is a business case waiting to be picked apart in the room. This skill separates the two before that happens, and refuses to let a genuine limitation get written up as a hidden strength.

## Gather the Inputs

- The call evidence: every team, role or use case the prospect described, in their own words
- Confirmed product capability: what the offer is actually documented or confirmed to do, not what seems like it should probably work
- Anything already known about how each team or role actually operates day to day

## Classify Each Use Case

For every team, role or use case raised, decide one of three things, and say why:

- **Good fit**: the confirmed need matches confirmed capability, directly, not by analogy.
- **Poor fit**: a specific, named mismatch exists, something the offer does not do, or something about how this team operates that the offer does not accommodate. State the mismatch plainly rather than softening it into a maybe.
- **Uncertain**: there is not yet enough evidence to call it either way. This is a real, distinct category, not a polite way of avoiding poor fit.

A single prospect can produce all three at once. Do not let a strong good-fit case anywhere in the same review pressure the others toward a more flattering answer.

## Apply the Guardrails

- Never turn a poor fit into a disguised strength. "Even better, because it forces adoption across the whole team at once" is exactly the move this skill exists to prevent when the real story is an unresolved integration or ownership gap.
- Never claim a capability that has not actually been confirmed, even when it seems like a reasonable extension of something the offer does do.
- Never let "uncertain" quietly become "good fit" because a decision is wanted sooner rather than later.
- Keep the reasoning specific to what was actually said or confirmed. "This team is probably fine" is not a classification; a stated reason is.

## Stop When the Task Is Unsafe

Do not produce a fit and limitations review when:

- No specific use case has actually been described, so there is nothing concrete to classify
- The request is to reclassify a poor fit as good because a business case is already being built around it
- Product capability itself is genuinely unclear even to the seller; say so and ask for the minimum confirmation needed rather than guessing

## Require Human Review

This produces a classification and the reasoning behind it. Deciding what to actually build a business case around, what to disqualify, and what to tell the prospect stays with the seller.
