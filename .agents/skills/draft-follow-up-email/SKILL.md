# Draft a Follow-Up Email

> Landed here directly rather than clicking through from a guide? This file is the instruction sheet an AI assistant follows, not written for a first read start to finish. [What is a sales AI skill?](../../../guides/what-is-a-sales-ai-skill.md) has the plain-English version.

Send one consistent template, personalised with real detail from the call. The template exists so the only decision left is what to personalise, not how to restructure the email.

## Gather the Inputs

Before writing, confirm:

- The call transcript or notes, and one or two specific real details worth using in the opening line
- Whether a business case or other resource already exists and has a live link. If it does not exist yet, build it first rather than sending a placeholder link.
- How many people this email is actually going to. One recipient changes the address form; more than one changes it more than that.
- That every link intended for this email is correct and working.

## Follow the Source Order

1. Direct statements from the call about what matters to this specific person
2. Confirmed detail already established in this conversation (a resource link, a date, a name)
3. The fixed template structure, which does not change regardless of what is being personalised

## Personalise It

- **Opening line**: the specific thing raised on the call, not a generic restatement such as "great speaking about your goals." If nothing specific can be pointed to in the transcript, go back and find something before writing.
- **Resource summary**: one sentence naming what the attached resource actually covers for this recipient, not template wording, unless the call genuinely gave nothing more specific.
- **Dates**: written with an ordinal suffix, for example 20th July, never 20 July or July 20th.
- **Named decision maker**: used by name if the call established one.
- **Links**: real hyperlinks on descriptive label text such as "Business Case for Alex" or "Book a Call". Never a bare URL, and never markdown link syntax in a context that will not render it, such as most CRM rich text fields.

## Handle the Template

Follow the fixed structure exactly. Do not reorder sections, merge sections, or rewrite the wording to make it "flow better". Every recurring failure on this task that was not a recipient-handling error was a template restructured under the belief that it read better that way. A fixed template earns its keep specifically because deviation is not a decision the drafting step gets to make.

## Handle Multiple Recipients

If the email is going to more than one person at the same company, the most common mistake is to pick one person as the addressee and describe the others in the third person. Address the group directly instead: everyone named is part of the "you" of the email, and if their situations differ, say so in a way that keeps them all included, rather than naming one reader and describing the rest as subjects.

If it is a single recipient, no other name should appear in the second person, and nobody else should be written as though they are party to the email.

## Apply the Guardrails

- Never refer to the primary recipient in the third person anywhere in the email, even when a manager or colleague is mentioned or copied. This is the single most repeated correction on this task; check it specifically before presenting.
- No em dashes, no emojis, no filler phrases such as "just circling back" or "hope you're well".
- Never send a placeholder link. If the resource is not ready, say so rather than sending the email early.

## Stop When the Task Is Unsafe

Do not produce a finished email when:

- The referenced resource (business case, brochure, cohort detail) does not actually exist yet
- No specific call detail is available to personalise the opening line
- It is unclear whether this is a single or multi-recipient email

Ask for the missing piece rather than guessing at it.

## Require Human Review

This drafts the email. Sending it, and any resulting CRM update, stays subject to explicit human approval.
