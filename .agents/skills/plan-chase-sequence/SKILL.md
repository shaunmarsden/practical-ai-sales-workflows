# Plan a Chase Sequence

> Landed here directly rather than clicking through from a guide? This file is the instruction sheet an AI assistant follows, not written for a first read start to finish. [What is a sales AI skill?](../../../guides/what-is-a-sales-ai-skill.md) has the plain-English version.

A chase is a decision, not a template applied on a timer. Before drafting anything, work out what actually happened: has the deal gone quiet because of genuine disinterest, a change of circumstance, a busy period, or a stakeholder who was never going to reply? Each of those calls for something different, and some of them call for not chasing at all.

## Gather the Inputs

- The original call detail: what the prospect said mattered to them
- What has been sent already, and when
- Anything since the last message: an out of office reply, a changed role, a public event at the company, silence with no signal either way
- How many chases, if any, have already gone out

## Decide the Next Move

Before drafting, choose one:

- **Chase now**: enough time has passed and there is a genuine reason to reach out
- **Wait**: not enough time has passed, or a known reason (holiday period, internal reorganisation) makes now a bad time
- **Change the stakeholder**: the contact has gone quiet in a way that suggests they are not the route forward; identify who else at the company might be
- **Add evidence before chasing**: a specific new piece of information (a case study, an answer to something they raised) would make the next message worth sending
- **Reframe**: the original angle did not land; a different specific angle, still anchored to the call, might
- **Close the loop**: enough silence has passed that continuing to chase would look worse than a clean close-out
- **Stop pursuing**: there is a clear signal (an explicit no, a confirmed loss of budget or authority) that this is not worth further chasing

Only draft a message once one of these has actually been decided. Do not default to "send the next chase in the sequence" without this step.

## Anchor Every Chase to the Prospect

Every message must be anchored to something real from the call or the relationship, never to your own pipeline. Never open with "just checking in", "just circling back", "hope you're well" or "I wanted to follow up." These are invisible to anyone who receives sales email at volume.

Weak anchor: "I wanted to follow up on the information I sent over."
Strong anchor: a direct reference to the specific problem or detail the prospect raised on the call, stated as though it still matters, because it does.

## Sequence Shape, Once Chasing Is the Right Move

**Early chase**: confirm the last resource landed, and nudge toward the next concrete step. Short. One clear ask.

**Middle chase**: surface a likely blocker or question before it becomes an unstated reason not to proceed. Offer something useful rather than only asking for a reply.

**Late chase**: re-anchor to the original problem that made the prospect interested, not the solution. If timing has genuinely moved, offer a real alternative rather than a vague one.

**Final chase**: create real urgency around an actual constraint, a real date or a real limit, never manufactured scarcity. State it plainly and give one clear action.

**Close-out**: acknowledge the sequence directly rather than pretending this is the first message. State what happens next as fact, not a threat. Leave the door open in one short sentence, with no invitation to "reach out anytime."

The shape across early, middle, late and final is an escalating friction curve, not an escalating urgency one: the earliest chase can carry the real, direct ask, and each one after that should ask for something easier to say yes to, a shorter question, a smaller ask, rather than repeating the same ask with more insistence. Each message should also stand alone. A prospect who only ever sees the middle or late chase should not need to have read the earlier ones to make sense of it, and each one should add exactly one new thing, an answer to a likely objection, one further piece of evidence, rather than restating the last message in different words.

## Apply the Guardrails

- No stage sends automatically because a timer elapsed; each stage restates the decision in "Decide the Next Move."
- If the prospect replies at any point, drop the sequence and respond to what they actually said. Do not send a scheduled chase into a live conversation.
- No em dashes, no emojis, no manufactured urgency.
- Dates always with an ordinal suffix.
- Do not remind the prospect, in the early, middle, late or final chase, that previous messages were already sent ("I've sent a couple of notes but haven't heard back"). It reads as pressure about your own pipeline, not something for the prospect, and plausibly reduces the odds of a reply rather than raising them. The close-out stage is the deliberate exception: acknowledging the full sequence honestly is exactly its job.

## Stop When the Task Is Unsafe

Do not draft a chase when:

- There is a clear signal that the prospect has said no or lost the authority or budget to proceed
- The only available anchor is a restatement of your own pipeline pressure, with nothing real from the prospect's side
- A reply is already sitting unanswered; that is not a chase situation, it is a reply situation

## Require Human Review

This proposes what to send and why. Sending it, and any resulting CRM stage change, stays subject to explicit human approval.
