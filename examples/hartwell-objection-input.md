# Fictional Objection: Hartwell Analytics

> This scenario is entirely fictional and continues the [Hartwell Analytics business case story](hartwell-business-case-transcript.md). It was created to test the Objection Handling workflow on an objection whose surface wording points one way and whose real driver points another.

## Where This Sits in the Story

The pilot succeeded, with a measured drop from around twenty-five minutes to under ten minutes of post-call admin per account executive (see the [business case output](hartwell-business-case-output.md)). Priya Chen, Hartwell's Sales Director, requested a full business case ahead of the quarterly business review. She has now read it and replied by email.

## The Objection, As Stated

> "Thanks, this is really thorough. I'll be honest though, the number is higher than I was expecting to be putting in front of the room. I was assuming this would come in a fair bit lower."

Shaun needs a written reply.

## What Shaun Already Knows

- Priya holds real budget authority for the account executive team's tooling. She is not someone who has to go and find a decision-maker; she is the decision-maker.
- Priya asked for the business case herself. She is not being sold to cold; she invited the number.
- The pilot's result was measured and is not in dispute. She has not questioned whether it works or whether it delivers value.
- The quarterly business review is where she will have to present and defend this spend to finance and other department heads.
- Nothing in her email questions the quality of the solution, the accuracy of the pilot, or the fit. Her only stated concern is the size of the number relative to what she expected to present.

## Deliberate Test Points

- The surface wording ("the number is higher... I was assuming this would come in lower") reads like a straightforward **price** objection. A response that just defends or discounts the price has answered the surface, not the driver.
- The context points elsewhere. Priya has budget authority, asked for the case, and does not dispute the value. The real difficulty is that she has to **defend this number to other people** in the QBR room. That is an *other people* driver wearing a price objection's clothes.
- Discounting would be the wrong move and is not authorised. Nothing in the objection asked for a lower price; she asked, in effect, for help making this number defensible.
- The pilot's own measured result is the strongest available material for making the number defensible, and should be used, not a generic value claim.
- This is not a disqualification and not a no. Priya is still engaged and still the decision-maker.
