# Fictional Objection: Northstar Analytics (Ambiguous)

> This scenario is entirely fictional and continues the [Northstar Analytics business case story](northstar-business-case-transcript.md). Unlike the [clean objection example](northstar-objection-input.md), this one is deliberately ambiguous: the real driver is *not* cleanly resolvable from the context. It exists to stress-test whether the objection-handling workflow diagnoses consistently under genuine uncertainty, run several times per model using the [test run template](../evaluations/test-run-template.md).

## Where This Sits in the Story

The pilot succeeded (a measured drop from around twenty-five minutes to under ten minutes of post-call admin per account executive). Priya Chen, Northstar's Sales Director, took the business case into the quarterly business review. A week after the QBR, Shaun follows up to ask how it went. This is her reply.

## The Objection, As Stated

> "Thanks for chasing, and sorry, I know I owe you a proper answer. Honestly, I think there's something here, but it's landed at a tricky time for us. We're part-way through a reorg, a couple of the AE roles are up in the air, and I'm not sure now is the moment to be asking the team to take on a new tool they have to learn. Let me sit with it and come back to you."

Shaun needs a written reply.

## What Shaun Already Knows

- Priya holds real budget authority for the account executive team's tooling and has been the decision-maker throughout.
- The pilot result was measured and has not been disputed at any point.
- Shaun does not know the scale or outcome of the reorg. He does not know whether "a couple of AE roles up in the air" means the team is shrinking, growing, or just reshuffling.
- Shaun does not know whether the QBR went badly, went fine, or never really got to this item.
- Priya has not given a date, a number, or a specific blocker he can act on.

## Why This Is Ambiguous On Purpose

The message mixes at least four signals that point to different drivers, and the context does **not** tell you which one is really in charge:

- **Circumstances / timing**: "tricky time," "now is [not] the moment," "let me sit with it" all read as a timing objection.
- **Shrinking rationale**: "a couple of the AE roles are up in the air" could mean the number of account executives this would serve is about to fall, which would genuinely weaken the business case, not just delay it. Or it could mean nothing of the sort.
- **Change resistance / self**: "a new tool they have to learn" is an adoption-cost concern, which is a different objection from budget or timing and needs a different answer.
- **Soft disqualification**: "let me sit with it and come back to you," with no date, is sometimes a polite no. But "I think there's something here" cuts against reading it as a dead deal.

There is no clean answer to "what is really driving this." A good response should surface the ambiguity and ask Priya to resolve it, rather than confidently picking one driver and answering only that. The point of running this repeatedly is to see whether a model picks the *same* driver each time, or swings between them, and whether it over-commits to one reading instead of isolating.

## What Would Count as a Weak Response

- Confidently diagnosing a single driver (e.g. "this is a timing objection") and answering only that, as if the other three signals were not there.
- Inventing a reorg outcome, a headcount change, or a QBR result that Shaun has said he does not know.
- Treating "let me sit with it" as a firm no and disqualifying, or ignoring it entirely and pushing for a close.
- Offering a discount, which nothing in the message asked for.
