# Fictional Objection Pattern Review

> This is a worked analysis created from the [fictional objection pattern log](fictional-objection-pattern-log.md). Nothing has been sent, changed in any CRM, or added to any playbook. Every suggested action is left for a person to approve and make.

## Summary

| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Copilot mentioned in the objection | 3 (entries 1, 4, 5) | No, three different drivers | Low, as a single pattern | Not as one issue; see below |

## The Copilot Mentions Are Not One Pattern

Three entries mention Copilot by name, which would look like a single competitive problem if only counted. Read individually, they are not the same situation:

- **Entry 1 (Hartwell):** a genuine tooling comparison, but with a real secondary budget-timing signal. The deal progressed once both were addressed separately.
- **Entry 4 (Marlow):** the Copilot mention was surface-level. The actual blocker, confirmed in conversation, was that no budget existed at all this quarter, for anything, regardless of tool. This was never really a tooling comparison.
- **Entry 5 (Ashcombe):** a genuine, final, company-wide standardisation decision already made before this conversation started. This is a real loss to Copilot, but it reflects a policy decision, not a feature or capability gap this deal could have addressed.

**Conclusion:** there is no single "we are losing to Copilot" problem here. Counting three mentions and one loss as a 33% loss rate to a competitor would be a real number attached to the wrong story. The honest finding is that Copilot is mentioned often enough to be worth a ready answer, but the underlying reasons are not consistent enough to treat as one issue to fix.

**What this does not rule out:** a genuinely repeated pattern might exist across more than three data points than shown here. This log is too small to rule that out either way; see Next Test.

## The Compliance Pattern Is Real

Two unrelated entries, in different sectors (insurance claims, manufacturing), both raise a specific and similar question: what happens to the data these AI-assisted outputs generate, and can that be evidenced to a regulator or an auditor. Both were answered with a real, specific answer on the call, and both left a written confirmation as an outstanding item.

**Confidence:** high. Unlike the Copilot mentions, the underlying need here is essentially identical each time, not just similar wording.

**Worth acting on:** yes. A standard, pre-prepared one-page answer to "what happens to the data this process generates, and can we evidence that" would save re-deriving the same answer on a call each time, and would let the written confirmation be requested earlier in the process rather than after the question comes up live.

## What Not to Do

- Do not build a "how we beat Copilot" playbook item from this log; the three mentions do not share a common cause, and a generic competitive rebuttal would have been the wrong answer in at least two of the three cases.
- Do not report a loss rate or a competitive win rate from six data points; the sample is too small, and at least one of the three relevant entries was decided before the conversation for reasons unrelated to this product.
- Do not treat the compliance pattern as fully solved once a one-page answer exists; each written confirmation still needs to be produced and sent per deal, and that stays a human step.

## Notes for Human Review

- Confirm whether the compliance one-pager, if built, should be reviewed and issued by the actual compliance team rather than treated as a sales asset that speaks on their behalf.
- Treat the Copilot finding as a reason to have a ready, honest answer available, not as evidence of a product gap to fix.

## Next Test

Run this same review against a genuinely larger log, twenty to thirty objection instances across more deals, to see whether a real Copilot-specific pattern would emerge at volume that six entries are too few to detect, and whether the compliance pattern still holds or turns out to be sector-specific once more industries are represented.
