# Fictional Example: Northstar Pre-Call Roleplay

> This is a short, illustrative roleplay exchange, created to show what the [pre-call objection roleplay prompt](../templates/pre-call-objection-roleplay-prompt.md) actually produces. It uses only the confirmed context and assumptions already established in the [Northstar pre-call example](northstar-pre-call.md), and pushes back along the "hypothesis is wrong or not a priority" path that card already anticipated.

## What Was Given to the Roleplay

Only the confirmed context, assumptions and opening already in the Northstar pre-call card: Alex Morgan, Head of Revenue Operations, reviewing how information moves between calls, email and the CRM; the admin-time hypothesis is explicitly labelled an assumption to test, not a confirmed fact.

## The Exchange

**Shaun (opening, as planned):** Alex, thanks for making the time. You mentioned that you're reviewing how information moves between calls, email and the CRM. I thought it would be useful to understand where that process currently creates the most work before we talk about any possible solution.

**"Alex" (roleplay):** Sure, though I'll be honest, this has been on the list for a while and it's not the thing keeping me up at night. We've got a few other projects ahead of it. What makes you think this is worth prioritising over what we're already doing?

**Shaun:** Fair question. I'm not assuming it should jump the queue, I'd rather understand where it actually sits. When you say it's been on the list a while, what's kept it from moving, has it just not been painful enough, or has something else kept getting in the way?

**"Alex" (roleplay):** Mostly the second one. Every time it comes up, something more urgent takes the slot. I don't know if that means it's not important or just never the loudest problem in the room.

**Shaun:** That's a useful distinction. If it turned out to be genuinely cheap to fix, would that change where it sits relative to the louder problems, or does it need to actually be causing pain somewhere specific before it's worth your time either way?

**"Alex" (roleplay):** If it were genuinely low effort, probably yes. I'd still want to see it's actually costing us something concrete, not just a tidiness thing.

## Debrief

- **What pushback actually came up:** a direct "why should this matter now" challenge, followed by an honest admission that the issue has never been the loudest problem, not that it has been dismissed outright.
- **What worked:** not arguing that the problem is urgent; instead asking what has kept it from moving, which opened up useful information (it is a real but quiet problem, not a rejected one) rather than provoking a defensive answer.
- **What to tighten before the real call:** the second response could go further and ask what "something concrete" would actually look like to Alex, since that is the exact evidence bar the real call will need to clear, and it was left open here rather than pinned down.
