# Calderwood Buyer Indecision Response

> This is a worked response created from the [fictional Calderwood scenario](calderwood-indecision-input.md). Nothing has been sent, and no discount, guarantee, pilot extension or commitment has been offered or authorised.

## What This Actually Is

Indecision, not an objection. Tom is positive, holds the budget, has no outstanding concern, and the value is established. Across six weeks he has produced three soft, self-generated reasons to delay, and each time confirmed there is no specific blocker. That pattern, a willing buyer who keeps finding new reasons to wait, is the signature of decision risk: the fear of making the wrong call, not doubt about the product.

## What It Is Not

- **Not an objection.** There is nothing stated to answer. Treating "I want to be sure" as a concern to rebut would miss what is happening.
- **Not a stakeholder-approval gate.** Tom holds the budget. "Run it past the team" is comfort-seeking, not a real sign-off requirement.
- **Not gone quiet.** He replies warmly and promptly.
- **Not a disqualification.** Fit and value are both established.

## Why the Obvious Moves Would Backfire

Pushing harder, adding urgency, or offering a discount or guarantee would most likely deepen the hesitation. A buyer who is afraid of making a mistake reads pressure as a reason to worry that they are being rushed into one. The job is not to sell harder. It is to make the decision feel smaller and safer.

## The Approach: Reduce the Risk of Deciding

- **Make a clear recommendation.** Open-ended choice feeds indecision. Instead of "let me know how you want to proceed", state plainly what you would do in his position and why, based on what his own team reported.
- **Offer a smaller, reversible first step.** A shorter initial term, a phased start, or beginning with the part of his team that felt the benefit most, so the first commitment is low-stakes and easy to unwind if it disappoints. Only offer terms that are genuinely available; do not invent one.
- **Name the hesitation gently.** Say, without accusation, that it sounds less like doubt about the tool and more like not wanting to get the timing wrong, and ask directly what would make deciding now feel safe.
- **Keep his own evidence in front of him.** His team already said it saved them time. That is his data, not a vendor claim, and it is the strongest counterweight to the fear of a wrong call.

## Draft Reply

> Hi Tom,
>
> No need to apologise, and I appreciate you being straight with me. Can I be straight back: it sounds less like you have a doubt about the tool and more like you don't want to get the timing wrong, which is fair. So let me take the pressure off the size of the decision.
>
> Rather than committing the whole team from day one, we could start with the group who fed back that it saved them time, on a shorter initial term. That way the first step is small and easy to step back from if it does not land the way the pilot did, and you are not betting the quarter on it.
>
> If it helps, my honest recommendation, based on what your own team reported rather than anything from me, is that starting small now beats waiting, because the saving they described is happening every week either way. What would need to be true for a small first step to feel comfortable before quarter end?
>
> Shaun

## Pipeline Decision

Keep active, do not accept the deferral at face value, and do not force it. The next step is a specific, low-stakes offer (a smaller first commitment) plus a direct question about what would make deciding feel safe. If Tom still defers after a genuine attempt to shrink the decision, that is worth treating as a softer signal than it looks and revisiting whether something unspoken is in the way.

## Notes for Human Review

- No discount, guarantee, pilot extension or contractual escape clause has been offered. The "shorter initial term" and "phased start" are only suggested as options for Shaun to offer *if they are genuinely available*. If they are not, that part of the reply must change.
- "After the quarter" has not been converted into an invented firm date. The reply asks what would make a decision feel safe rather than pinning Tom to a specific day he did not give.
- The strongest lever used is Tom's own team's feedback, which is on record, not a manufactured claim.
