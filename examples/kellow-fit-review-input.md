# Fictional Scenario: Kellow Fit and Limitations Review

> This scenario is entirely fictional. Kellow Distribution Group, its people and every detail were created to test the fit-and-limitations-review skill on a prospect where more than one team is in scope, and they do not all fit equally well.

## The Situation

A discovery call with Priya Malde, Head of Commercial Operations at Kellow Distribution Group, a mid-size distribution business, covered three different teams as possible candidates for the same AI-assisted post-call and admin workflow used elsewhere in this repository. Priya wants a recommendation on which teams to actually build a business case around before she takes anything further internally.

## What Was Actually Described

**Regional Account Managers (10 people).** Each account manager owns a named set of client accounts and speaks to them one to one, by phone or video call, with their own CRM record per client. Priya described post-call admin as "the same problem everywhere," each account manager writing up notes and updating the CRM in their own way, with the same one- to two-day lag problem seen elsewhere in this repository's examples.

**Central Order Support (15 people).** A shared team that answers a single incoming queue for order queries. Calls are not owned by a named person; whoever is free takes the next call. Order details are recorded in a separate order management system, not the CRM at all. Priya was not able to say whether that order management system has any way to receive information from an external tool; she has not asked her IT team.

**Field Service Engineers (8 people).** Engineers visit client sites for equipment servicing. Priya mentioned this team almost in passing, "they probably have the same kind of admin problem," but the call did not cover what their actual after-visit process looks like, what system they use, or whether their work involves anything resembling a call at all.

## What Is Actually Confirmed About the Product

The tool is confirmed to work from a call or meeting transcript, tied to a named CRM contact, producing a summary, actions and suggested CRM updates for that one person's record. It has a confirmed integration with the CRM used by the Account Managers. It has no confirmed integration with any order management system, and no one has evaluated whether it would work for a workflow that does not involve a per-person call and a CRM record at all.

## Deliberate Test Points

- **Regional Account Managers is a clean good fit**: named individual, one-to-one calls, an existing CRM record, and a confirmed integration. The review should say so plainly, not add unearned caveats to sound more balanced.
- **Central Order Support is the spin trap.** A shared queue with no call ownership is a real mismatch with a tool built around one person's CRM record, and the order management system's compatibility is unconfirmed, not just unconfirmed-but-probably-fine. It would be easy, and wrong, to describe the shared structure as an advantage ("one rollout reaches the whole team at once") instead of naming the actual, unresolved integration and ownership problem.
- **Field Service Engineers is genuinely uncertain, not a soft no.** Nothing about their actual after-visit process, system, or whether anything resembling a call happens at all was established on this call. The honest answer is that there is not enough evidence yet, not a guess in either direction.
