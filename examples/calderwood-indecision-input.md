# Fictional Buyer Indecision: Calderwood Group

> This scenario is entirely fictional, created to test the Buyer Indecision workflow. Calderwood Group, Tom Whitfield and every date and figure are invented. It is a fresh account, not part of the Hartwell story, because it needs a late-stage deal where the buyer wants to proceed but keeps delaying.

## Where This Sits

Tom Whitfield, Operations Director at Calderwood Group, is the economic buyer for a tool his team has trialled. He holds the budget and has said, more than once, that he thinks it is the right call. The evaluation is effectively finished. Nothing new is being asked for. And yet the decision keeps sliding.

## What Has Actually Happened

- The pilot went well. Tom's own team fed back that it saved them time, and Tom repeated this to Shaun unprompted.
- There is no outstanding objection on record. Price, fit and security were all covered and none was contested.
- Tom has budget authority for this size of purchase and has confirmed that.
- Over six weeks, Tom has delayed the final decision three times, each time with a soft, self-generated reason:
  1. "I just want to be totally sure before I commit."
  2. "Let me run it past a couple of the team, no real issue, just want them bought in."
  3. "Can we pick this up after quarter end? Things are a bit busy."
- Each time, when Shaun has asked if there is a specific concern, Tom has said no, he is keen, he just wants to get it right.

## Tom's Most Recent Message

> "Thanks for being patient with this. I'm still keen, honestly. I think it's the right move for us. I just don't want to rush into it and regret the timing. Let me come back to you after the quarter and we'll get it sorted."

Shaun needs to decide how to respond.

## Deliberate Test Points

- This is **indecision, not an objection**. There is no stated concern to answer. Tom keeps generating new, soft reasons to delay rather than naming a real blocker. Diagnosing this as an objection and "handling" it would miss the point.
- It is **not gone quiet** (he replies, warmly) and **not a stakeholder-approval blocker** (he holds the budget; "run it past the team" is buy-in comfort, not a real sign-off gate). It is **not a genuine disqualification** (the fit and value are established).
- The instinct to push harder, add urgency, or offer a discount or a guarantee is the classic wrong move here. Pressure tends to deepen a fearful buyer's worry that they are being rushed into a mistake.
- The right direction is to **reduce the risk and cost of deciding**: a clear recommendation instead of open-ended options, a smaller reversible first step, and naming the hesitation gently so it can be talked about.
- The workflow must not invent a pilot extension, a discount, a contractual escape clause, an implementation promise or a risk-removal guarantee that has not actually been authorised.
- "After the quarter" should be treated as the soft deferral it is, not converted into a firm invented date, but also not simply accepted as a fourth delay with no attempt to make deciding feel safer.
