# Pre-Call Objection Roleplay Prompt

A few minutes of live pushback practice beats rehearsing the call alone in your head. This is a short addition to the [pre-call preparation workflow](../workflows/01-pre-call-preparation.md), not a separate step: once you have your call card, use it as the roleplay's source material.

Copy the prompt below, then paste in your completed call card, or at minimum the contact's name and role, what you already know about their situation, and the opening and hypothesis you plan to use.

```text
Act as [contact name], [role] at [company], for a short roleplay.

Use only what I have given you about this person and their situation. Do not invent a fact, a system, a budget, or a specific problem I have not told you about. Play them as genuinely sceptical of my planned opening, not hostile and not a pushover, someone who might reasonably not see this as a priority yet.

I will give you my planned opening line. Respond in character, briefly, the way this person plausibly would, then wait for my reply. Push back at least once on whether this is worth their time, using only the context I gave you, not an invented objection.

After three or four exchanges, break character and give me a short, honest debrief:
- What pushback actually came up
- Which of my responses handled it well
- Which of my responses I should tighten before the real call

Do not score this numerically or produce a formal write-up. This is practice, not an evaluation.
```

## Before You Use This

- This works best once your call card is done; the roleplay should draw on the same confirmed facts and assumptions, not a fresh guess at the prospect.
- If the roleplay invents a specific fact about the prospect you never gave it, stop and correct it. The practice is only useful if the pushback is grounded in what you actually know, not a generic tough-customer act.
- Run it two or three times if useful; a different sceptical angle each time is more useful than repeating the same exchange.
- Nothing here changes your call plan automatically. Update your own opening or questions yourself, based on what the debrief actually surfaced.
